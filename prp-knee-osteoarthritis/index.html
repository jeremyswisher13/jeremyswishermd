Knee osteoarthritis · PRP guide

PRP for knee arthritis, explained beyond the label.

Platelet-rich plasma can improve pain and function for some people with knee osteoarthritis, but PRP is not one standardized product. Preparation, total platelet delivery, patient selection, and the rehabilitation plan all matter.

Start here

A blood-derived product, not a brand name.

PRP is made by drawing blood, concentrating platelets in a smaller volume of plasma, and injecting the prepared product into the knee. It is not a stem-cell treatment, and the letters “PRP” do not tell you the dose or full composition.

bloodtype

What it is

An autologous blood product that contains concentrated platelets and signaling proteins.

directions_walk

What it may do

Reduce symptoms and improve function for a period of time in selected patients.

warning

What it cannot promise

Cartilage regrowth, disease reversal, or avoidance of future knee replacement.

Current evidence

Benefit is possible, but response is not uniform.

A 2025 meta-analysis of 18 placebo-controlled trials involving 1,995 patients found clinically meaningful improvement in WOMAC scores across follow-up, with pain improvement most evident at three and six months. Higher platelet concentration was associated with better results.

That pooled result does not erase important negative trials. In the 288-patient RESTORE trial, three weekly leukocyte-poor PRP injections did not improve 12-month pain or medial tibial cartilage volume compared with saline. Other sham-controlled trials have reported benefit. The honest conclusion is that some PRP protocols help some patients, while the product and the responder remain incompletely defined.

Pooled signal

Across many trials, average pain and function outcomes generally favor PRP over placebo or common comparators.

Rigorous counterweight

Several well-designed saline-controlled studies have found little or no benefit, including RESTORE.

Clinical meaning

A favorable average does not predict an individual response, and the preparation used in one study may not match another.

tune

Why apparently similar trials can disagree

Studies vary in platelet dose, injected volume, leukocytes and neutrophils, red blood cell contamination, activation, number and timing of injections, image guidance, rehabilitation, osteoarthritis severity, and outcome measurement.

Product quality

PRP is a dose, not just a label.

A statement such as “four times concentrated” is incomplete unless the starting count, final concentration, and injected volume are known. Total deliverable platelets provide a more useful description of what reaches the knee.

calculate

Total platelet dose

Final platelets per microliter × injected volume in milliliters × 1,000 = total deliverable platelets.

For example, 1.25 million platelets per microliter in 8 mL yields 10 billion total platelets.

Core dose

Final platelet concentration, injected volume, and calculated total deliverable platelets.

Cell composition

Leukocytes, neutrophils, and red blood cell contamination can distinguish biologically different products.

Protocol

Preparation method, activation, device settings, injection number, guidance, and rehabilitation plan.

Why the 10-billion target deserves attention

  1. 01

    Concentration signal

    The 2025 Bensa meta-analysis found greater clinical improvement in higher-concentration PRP groups, although concentration is not the same as total dose.

  2. 02

    Direct randomized signal

    A 2024 trial reported better three- and six-month outcomes with about 5.65 billion platelets in 8 mL than with about 2.82 billion in 4 mL. Dose and volume changed together, so the trial does not isolate platelet dose alone.

  3. 03

    Ten-billion clinical signal

    A 2021 randomized study found that a leukocyte-depleted product delivering about 10 billion platelets produced better 12-month outcomes than hyaluronic acid. It was not a dose-comparison trial and did not establish a minimum effective dose.

  4. 04

    Current best-practice guidance

    AAPM&R's 2026 guidance recommends at least 10 billion total deliverable platelets as a best-practice target for knee osteoarthritis.

Bottom line

Ten billion is an evidence-informed target, not a proven biological cutoff.

Higher delivery may matter, and current AAPM&R guidance now supports a target of at least 10 billion platelets. The available trials do not prove that 9.9 billion cannot work, that 10 billion will work, or that more is always better.

Questions worth asking about any PRP product

  • What is being delivered? Ask for final platelet concentration, injected volume, and total platelet dose.
  • What else is in it? Ask whether leukocytes, neutrophils, and red blood cell contamination are characterized.
  • Why this schedule? Ask why one injection or a series is being recommended for this diagnosis.
  • How will it be judged? Define the functional goal, reassessment timing, rehabilitation plan, and total cost.

Patient selection

Who may reasonably consider PRP?

PRP is generally considered after the diagnosis is clear and foundational treatment has not provided enough relief. The decision should start with the knee and the patient's goals, not with the availability of an injection.

check_circle

More evidence aligned

Persistent symptomatic mild-to-moderate knee osteoarthritis after an appropriate exercise, rehabilitation, and symptom-management plan.

flag

Realistic treatment goal

Improve pain and function enough to support meaningful activity, while understanding that nonresponse is possible.

pause_circle

Lower confidence or defer

End-stage disease, active infection, a hot unexplained effusion, major diagnostic uncertainty, or expectations of cartilage regrowth.

Age, disease severity, alignment, metabolic health, medications, prior treatment, and activity demands may influence the discussion. None serves as a perfect responder test.

Before, during, and after

A reproducible protocol matters.

  1. 01

    Confirm the pain source

    The visit begins with history, examination, and review of appropriate imaging. A new hot effusion or major diagnostic uncertainty should be evaluated before an elective injection.

  2. 02

    Review safety and medication instructions

    Discuss infection risk, blood conditions, anticoagulants, prior reactions, and any instructions from the care team. Do not stop prescription medication on your own.

  3. 03

    Prepare and deliver the product

    Blood is drawn and processed, the skin is cleaned, and the PRP is injected. Ultrasound guidance may be used to visualize the target and needle.

  4. 04

    Progress activity deliberately

    Temporary soreness or swelling is common. The care team provides activity and rehabilitation instructions, then reassesses symptoms and function over time.

event_repeat

One injection or a series?

The best number and spacing of injections remain unsettled. More injections are not automatically better. Product, evidence, burden, cost, response, and the overall treatment plan should guide the choice.

emergency

When to seek urgent care after an injection

Promptly contact the clinical team for rapidly worsening pain, marked swelling, spreading redness, drainage, fever, or feeling ill. Severe symptoms may require urgent or emergency assessment to exclude infection or another complication.

Guideline context

Recommendations have evolved, but they still differ.

Professional groups review different evidence, use different thresholds for clinical importance, and publish at different times. The date and scope of a recommendation matter.

AAPM&R, 2026

Recommends considering PRP after failed conservative care in mild-to-moderate knee osteoarthritis, with product characterization and a target of at least 10 billion platelets.

AAOS, 2021

States that PRP may reduce pain and improve function, but rates the recommendation as limited.

VA/DoD, 2026

Finds insufficient evidence to recommend for or against PRP because the research remains heterogeneous and very uncertain.

ESSKA-ICRS, 2024

Supports selected use after conservative care has failed in patients with KL grade 0 to 3 disease, but not as first treatment or for KL grade 4.

Guidelines inform the conversation. They do not replace individual diagnosis, medical screening, cost discussion, and shared decision-making.

Cost and alternatives

An injection should compete with the whole plan.

PRP is often not covered by insurance and may involve substantial out-of-pocket cost. Coverage, authorization, product availability, and pricing should be confirmed with the clinic and health plan before treatment.

  • Foundational care. Education, progressive exercise, rehabilitation, activity planning, and weight-management support when relevant.
  • Medication options. Topical or oral medicines when appropriate for the patient's health history.
  • Other injections. Corticosteroid or hyaluronic acid may be discussed selectively, with different evidence, timing, risks, and coverage.
  • Surgical consultation. Appropriate when substantial symptoms and limitations persist or advanced disease changes the balance of options.

For PTs & referring clinicians

Keep the biologic product connected to rehabilitation.

Useful referral information includes symptom behavior, functional goals, rehabilitation dosage and response, objective progress, prior procedures, imaging, and the reason an injection is being considered. If PRP is performed, the post-procedure progression should be coordinated with the treating therapist.

Common questions

PRP for knee arthritis FAQs

Is PRP proven to regrow knee cartilage?

No. PRP is considered a symptom-modifying treatment. Current evidence does not establish reliable cartilage regeneration, reversal of osteoarthritis, or avoidance of future knee replacement.

Does a 10-billion platelet dose guarantee success?

No. AAPM&R's 2026 guidance recommends at least 10 billion total deliverable platelets as a best-practice target, but the value is not a proven universal threshold and does not predict an individual response.

Is leukocyte-poor or leukocyte-rich PRP better?

For knee osteoarthritis, many protocols favor leukocyte-poor products, but preparation methods and reporting remain inconsistent. Total dose and other cellular characteristics should be considered together rather than relying on one label.

How quickly does PRP work?

When benefit occurs, it is usually assessed over weeks to months rather than immediately. Temporary soreness in the first several days is common. The timing and degree of improvement vary.

Can I schedule PRP directly?

Call UCLA Orthopedics to arrange an evaluation with Dr. Swisher. Candidacy, product availability, cost, medication instructions, and procedure timing must be reviewed first, so an injection is not guaranteed at the initial visit.
